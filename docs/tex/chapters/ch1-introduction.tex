\chapter{Introduction} \label{c1:intro}

\section{Background} \label{sec:background}

Scientific publications build upon previously published work by referencing related research.
If a publication $y$ refers to another publication $x$, we say that $y$ cites $x$, and the reference is called a citation.

Citations form a directed relationship between publications and induce a citation network, where nodes correspond to publications, and edges correspond to citation links.
This network is widely used to assess the impact and relevance of scientific work.

For a given publication $x$, the \emph{number of citations} it receives is the number of distinct publications that reference it.
The citation counts can be aggregated per author to estimate the influence of a researcher.

Not all citations carry the same meaning.
In particular, citations can be divided into two main categories:

\begin{itemize}

\item
\emph{Self-citations} - where at least one author of the citing publication is also an author of the cited publication.

\item
\emph{External citations} - where there is no overlap between the authors of the citing and cited publications.

\end{itemize}

This distinction is important because external citations are typically considered a stronger indicator of influence, as they reflect recognition from independent researchers.
In contrast, self-citations may arise naturally in cumulative research but can also inflate citation-based metrics if used excessively.


\section{Problem statement} \label{sec:problem-statement}

The objective of this work is to create a tool that can analyse citation relationships at the author level and to distinguish between different types of citations.

Given a specific author, we want to:

\begin{itemize}

\item
identify their publications

\item
compute the total number of citations received by these publications

\item
distinguish between self-citations and external citations

\end{itemize}

A naive aggregation of citation counts does not capture this distinction and may provide a misleading estimate of research impact.
In particular, publications with a high number of self-citations may appear influential even when their external recognition is limited.

To address this limitation, this work focuses on the identification and classification of citations based on author overlap, providing a more appropriate (accurate/objective?) interpretation of citation metrics.

\section{Example} \label{sec:intro-example}

To demonstrate the distinction between self-citations and external citations, consider the following example.

Let publication $P_1$ be authored by researchers $A$, $B$ and $C$.
A second publication $P_2$, authored by $A$ and $D$, includes a reference to $P_1$.
In this case, the citation from $P_2$ to $P_1$ is a \emph{self-citation}, since the author $A$ appears in both publications.

Now let us consider a third publication $P_3$, authored by researchers $E$ and $F$; it also cites $P_1$.
As there is no overlap between the authors of $P_3$ and $P_1$, this citation is classified as an \emph{external citation}.

This example illustrates how the classification of citations depends on the relationship between the authors of the citing and cited publications.
